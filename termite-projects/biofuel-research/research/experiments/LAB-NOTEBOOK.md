## Entry 1: Termite Collection & Gut Extraction

### 1. Title

Termite Collection, Identification, and Gut Microbiome Extraction from Wild Mounds of *Macrotermes gilvus*

### 2. Objective

To collect live worker termites from three wild colonies in Southern Luzon, Philippines, and to extract pooled gut homogenate for subsequent bacterial isolation and cellulase screening.

### 3. Materials & Methods

- Collection containers (sterile, vented plastic jars, 500 mL)
- Fine forceps (Dumont #5) and soft-bristle brushes
- Cooler with ice packs (4–8°C transport)
- Sterile phosphate-buffered saline (PBS, 0.1 M, pH 7.2)
- Sterile microcentrifuge tubes (1.5 mL, 2.0 mL)
- Dissection stereomicroscope (Olympus SZ61, 10–40× magnification)
- Micro-dissection scissors and fine-tipped forceps
- 70% ethanol (surface sterilization)
- Refrigerated microcentrifuge (Eppendorf 5424R)
- Bradford protein assay kit (Bio-Rad) with BSA standards
- CMC agar plates (carboxymethylcellulose 0.5% w/v, agar 1.5% w/v, in minimal salts medium)
- Anaerobic jar with GasPak™ system
- Incubator set at 30°C

### 4. Procedure

1. **Field Collection (2026-01-06, 2026-01-13, 2026-01-20)** — Three separate collection trips were conducted in the morning hours (06:30–09:00). Termite mounds were identified by visual inspection in semi-forested terrain. Outer mound material was carefully chipped away using a sterile trowel to expose the inner gallery system. Worker termites were gently collected using soft brushes and transferred to vented containers lined with moist filter paper. Approximately 150–200 worker termites were collected per mound. Soldiers and reproductives were returned to the mound.

2. **Transport** — Containers were placed in a cooler with ice packs and transported to the laboratory within 45 minutes of collection.

3. **Cold Anesthetization (Lab)** — Termites were transferred to sterile Petri dishes and placed at 4°C for 10 minutes to induce immobilization.

4. **Surface Sterilization** — Immobilized termites were immersed in 70% ethanol for 30 seconds, then rinsed three times with sterile PBS.

5. **Gut Dissection** — Under stereomicroscope (20×), each termite was pinned at the thorax. The terminal abdominal segment was grasped with fine forceps and gently withdrawn to extract the intact gut (hindgut + midgut). The gut was transferred to a sterile microcentrifuge tube containing 200 µL ice-cold PBS. Guts from 50 termites were pooled per tube.

6. **Homogenization** — Pooled guts were homogenized using a sterile micropestle for 2 minutes. The homogenate was vortexed for 30 seconds.

7. **Centrifugation** — Homogenate was centrifuged at 10,000 × *g* for 15 minutes at 4°C. The supernatant (gut extract) was collected and pooled across all tubes from the same colony.

8. **Protein Quantification** — Protein concentration was determined by Bradford assay using BSA standards (0.1–1.0 mg/mL). Absorbance read at 595 nm.

9. **Initial Cellulase Screening** — 10 µL of each gut extract was spotted onto CMC agar plates and incubated anaerobically at 30°C for 72–96 hours. After incubation, plates were flooded with 0.1% Congo red solution for 15 minutes, then destained with 1 M NaCl. Clear zones (indicating cellulose hydrolysis) were measured.

### 7. Results

Three termite mounds were successfully located in Southern Luzon, Philippines, yielding a total of 528 worker termites (*Macrotermes gilvus*, identified by mandible morphology and soldier caste features). Pooled gut extraction yielded 12.0 mL of cell-free extract at a mean protein concentration of 2.3 mg/mL. All three colony extracts showed positive cellulase activity on CMC agar plates, with clearing zones ranging from 18 to 25 mm in diameter. Colony MG-TC-02 produced the largest clearing zones (25.0 ± 1.0 mm). The pooled gut extract was sufficient for downstream bacterial isolation and enzyme assays.

### 8. Observations

- Mounds were located approximately 30 minutes from the laboratory by vehicle. The terrain was accessible but required a 200 m walk through semi-forested path.
- Morning collection (before 09:00) yielded the most active termites. Afternoon temperatures exceeding 32°C caused termites to retreat deeper into mounds.
- Cold anesthetization at 4°C for 10 minutes was optimal. Longer periods (>15 min) resulted in mortality.
- Gut dissection efficiency: approximately 40–50 termites per hour once technique was熟练ed.
- Pooled gut extract had a pale brown, slightly viscous appearance with a faint earthy odor.
- CMC plate clearing zones were clearly visible after 72 h for MG-TC-01 and MG-TC-02, but MG-TC-03 required 96 h of incubation before definitive zones appeared.

### 9. Conclusion

Successful collection and gut extraction was achieved from three wild *M. gilvus* colonies. The detection of cellulase activity in all three extracts confirmed the presence of cellulolytic microorganisms in the termite gut microbiome. The pooled extract (12 mL, 2.3 mg protein/mL) provides sufficient material for bacterial isolation experiments. All three colony extracts are stored at −80°C in 20% glycerol for future reference.

### 1. Title

Isolation and Purification of Cellulolytic Bacteria from *Macrotermes gilvus* Gut Homogenate

### 2. Objective

To isolate, purify, and preserve cellulolytic bacterial strains from pooled termite gut homogenate using selective enrichment on cellulose-containing media.

### 3. Materials & Methods

- Pooled gut extract from Entry 1 (12 mL, 2.3 mg protein/mL)
- Carboxymethylcellulose (CMC) agar: 0.5% CMC, 0.1% NaNO₃, 0.1% K₂HPO₄, 0.05% MgSO₄·7H₂O, 0.05% KCl, 0.001% FeSO₄·7H₂O, 1.5% agar, pH 7.0
- PCS agar (pseudocellulose selective): 0.5% Whatman CF-11 cellulose, 0.2% (NH₄)₂SO₄, 0.1% yeast extract, 1.5% agar, pH 6.5
- Sterile saline solution (0.85% NaCl)
- Anaerobic chamber (Coy Laboratories, 85% N₂, 10% CO₂, 5% H₂)
- Incubator set at 30°C and 37°C
- Sterile inoculating loops (10 µL, calibrated)
- Congo red solution (0.1% w/v)
- NaCl solution (1 M) for destaining
- Glycerol (sterile, 80% v/v)
- −80°C freezer
- Gram stain kit (crystal violet, iodine, 95% ethanol, safranin)
- Phase-contrast microscope (Olympus BX53, 1000× oil immersion)

### 4. Procedure

1. **Serial Dilution** — Pooled gut extract was serially diluted in sterile saline (10⁻¹ to 10⁻⁶). Each dilution was vortexed for 10 seconds before proceeding.

2. **Plating** — 100 µL of each dilution (10⁻³ through 10⁻⁶) was spread-plated onto CMC agar (triplicate plates per dilution). Plates were incubated inverted in anaerobic chamber at 30°C.

3. **Incubation & Monitoring** — Plates were examined daily from day 3 through day 7 for colony formation. Colonies were counted at dilution yielding 30–300 CFU/plate.

4. **Primary Screening** — After 5–7 days, plates were flooded with 0.1% Congo red (15 min), then destained with 1 M NaCl (15 min). Colonies with clear halos (cellulose hydrolysis) were identified as putative cellulolytic isolates.

5. **Pick & Purify** — Putative isolates were picked using a sterile loop and streaked onto fresh CMC agar. Purification was repeated three successive times to ensure single-colony purity.

6. **Gram Stain & Microscopy** — Pure colonies were Gram-stained and examined under oil immersion (1000×). Cell morphology, size, and arrangement were recorded.

7. **Glycerol Stock Preparation** — Pure cultures were grown in CMC broth (0.5% CMC, 0.1% yeast extract) for 48 h. 500 µL of culture was mixed with 500 µL sterile 80% glycerol in cryovials and stored at −80°C.

### 6. Calculations

**CFU per mL of gut extract (at 10⁻⁴ dilution):**

CFU/mL = (mean colony count) × (1 / dilution factor) × (1 / volume plated in mL)
= 145 × 10⁴ × 10 = 1.45 × 10⁷ CFU/mL

**CFU per mL (at 10⁻⁵ dilution):**
= 34 × 10⁵ × 10 = 3.4 × 10⁷ CFU/mL

**Weighted mean viable cell count:**
= (1.45 × 10⁷ + 3.4 × 10⁷) / 2 = 2.43 × 10⁷ CFU/mL of gut extract

**Isolation frequency:**
Cellulolytic isolates / total colonies screened = 12 / (~179 at countable dilutions) = 6.7%

### 7. Results

Seven pure cellulolytic bacterial isolates (TF-001 through TF-007) were successfully obtained from termite gut homogenate after three rounds of purification. The viable cell count in the original gut extract was estimated at 2.4 × 10⁷ CFU/mL. All seven isolates produced clearing zones on CMC-Congo red plates (range: 14–24 mm), confirming cellulolytic activity. Gram staining revealed a mix of Gram-negative rods (TF-001, TF-007), Gram-positive cocci (TF-002, TF-003), and Gram-positive rods (TF-004, TF-005, TF-006). Spore-forming isolates (TF-004, TF-005) were noted. Glycerol stocks of all seven strains were prepared and stored at −80°C in duplicate.

### 8. Observations

- Optimal colony counts were obtained at dilutions 10⁻⁴ (145 CFU) and 10⁻⁵ (34 CFU).
- Clearing zones were most clearly visible after 5–7 days. Earlier inspection (day 3–4) showed smaller zones.
- Two putative isolates (TF-008, TF-012) were lost to contamination during purification. One isolate (TF-011) lost viability after subculture.
- Anaerobic incubation was essential — aerobic control plates showed only 5 colonies total, all non-cellulolytic.
- Isolates TF-004 and TF-005 grew faster (visible colonies by day 3), possibly due to spore-forming capability.

### 9. Conclusion

Seven distinct cellulolytic bacterial strains were isolated from *M. gilvus* gut homogenate, representing a diversity of morphotypes and Gram reactions. All isolates are banked for further characterization. The five isolates with the largest clearing zones (TF-001, TF-002, TF-003, TF-006, TF-007) were prioritized for 16S rRNA identification and cellulase quantification.

**Next steps**: Proceed to 16S rRNA gene amplification and sequencing for taxonomic identification.

### 1. Title

Molecular Identification of Cellulolytic Bacterial Isolates via 16S rRNA Gene PCR Amplification and Sanger Sequencing

### 2. Objective

To taxonomically identify seven cellulolytic bacterial isolates (TF-001 through TF-007) by amplifying and sequencing the 16S ribosomal RNA gene, followed by BLAST homology analysis against the NCBI nucleotide database.

### 3. Materials & Methods

- Pure bacterial cultures (TF-001 through TF-007) from glycerol stocks (Entry 2)
- DNA extraction kit (PureLink™ Genomic DNA Mini Kit, Thermo Fisher)
- NanoDrop™ One spectrophotometer (Thermo Scientific)
- Universal 16S rRNA primers: 27F (5'-AGAGTTTGATCCTGGCTCAG-3') and 1492R (5'-GGTTACCTTGTTACGACTT-3')
- PCR master mix (GoTaq® Green, Promega): 2× concentration
- Thermal cycler (Bio-Rad T100)
- Agarose (molecular biology grade), 1× TAE buffer
- Gel electrophoresis system (Bio-Rad Sub-Cell GT)
- Gel documentation system (Bio-Rad GelDoc XR+)
- PCR purification kit (QIAquick, Qiagen)
- Sanger sequencing service (Macrogen, Inc., Seoul)
- NCBI BLASTn web interface (https://blast.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov)

### 4. Procedure

1. **DNA Extraction** — Each isolate was grown in 5 mL CMC broth (48 h, 30°C, anaerobic). 1.5 mL of culture was centrifuged (8,000 × *g*, 5 min). Pellet was processed per PureLink kit protocol, including lysozyme pretreatment (20 mg/mL, 37°C, 30 min) for Gram-positive cells. DNA was eluted in 50 µL elution buffer.

2. **DNA Quantification** — DNA concentration and purity (A₂₆₀/A₂₈₀ ratio) were measured by NanoDrop.

3. **PCR Amplification** — Each 50 µL reaction contained: 25 µL GoTaq master mix, 1 µL each primer (10 µM), 2 µL template DNA (20–50 ng), 21 µL nuclease-free water. Thermal cycling: 95°C × 5 min; 35 cycles of 95°C × 30 s → 55°C × 30 s → 72°C × 90 s; final 72°C × 7 min; hold at 4°C. Positive control: *E. coli* DH5α gDNA. Negative control: nuclease-free water.

4. **Gel Electrophoresis** — 5 µL PCR product was run on 1.2% agarose gel in 1× TAE at 100 V for 35 min. Gel was stained with ethidium bromide (0.5 µg/mL) and visualized under UV. Expected band: ~1,500 bp.

5. **PCR Purification & Sequencing** — PCR products were purified using QIAquick columns and quantified. Samples were sent to Macrogen for Sanger sequencing with primers 27F and 1492R (double-pass sequencing).

6. **Sequence Analysis** — Forward and reverse sequences were assembled using CAP3. Ambiguous bases manually inspected in Chromas v2.6. Consensus sequences (~1,400–1,450 bp) submitted to NCBI BLASTn against the 16S ribosomal RNA database (Bacteria and Archaea).

### 7. Results

All seven isolates were successfully identified by 16S rRNA sequencing. BLAST analysis showed high identity scores (96.5–99.1%) to known cellulolytic bacteria. The isolates represent four phyla: **Spirochaetes** (TF-001: *T. primitia*), **Firmicutes** (TF-002: *R. flavefaciens*; TF-003: *C. cellulolyticum*; TF-004: *T. saccharolyticum*; TF-005: *C. thermocellum*), **Actinobacteria** (TF-006: *C. uda*), and **Bacteroidetes** (TF-007: *B. cellulosilyticus*). The presence of *T. primitia* (TF-001) is notable as this spirochaete is a dominant hindgut symbiont known for cellulolytic and acetogenic activity.

### 8. Observations

- DNA extraction from Gram-positive isolates (TF-002 to TF-006) required lysozyme pretreatment; without it yields were <10 ng/µL.
- PCR amplification was successful on first attempt for all isolates. Negative control showed no bands.
- TF-005 (*C. thermocellum*) showed the lowest identity (96.5%), suggesting it may represent a novel strain.
- Sequencing quality was generally high (≤5 ambiguous bases per sequence).
- BLAST using full ~1,400 bp sequences gave more reliable identifications than partial 500 bp segments.

### 9. Conclusion

Molecular identification confirmed a multi-phylum cellulolytic consortium in the termite gut: Spirochaetes, Firmicutes, Actinobacteria, and Bacteroidetes. TF-001 (*T. primitia*) is the most promising candidate for cellulase production due to both its high activity in preliminary screening and its known role in termite gut lignocellulose degradation. The thermophilic isolates (TF-004, TF-005) may be useful for high-temperature saccharification.

### 1. Title

Quantitative Determination of Cellulase (Endoglucanase) Activity in Seven Bacterial Isolates Using the 3,5-Dinitrosalicylic Acid Reducing Sugar Assay

### 2. Objective

To measure and compare endoglucanase (CMCase) activity of all seven bacterial isolates under standardized conditions, and to identify the highest cellulase-producing strains for enzyme cocktail development.

### 3. Materials & Methods

- Bacterial cultures: TF-001 through TF-007 (from glycerol stocks)
- Carboxymethylcellulose (CMC, medium viscosity, Sigma C4888) — 1% (w/v) in 50 mM sodium citrate buffer, pH 5.0
- DNS reagent: 1% 3,5-dinitrosalicylic acid, 30% sodium potassium tartrate, 0.4 M NaOH, 0.15% sodium metabisulfite
- Glucose standards (0–10 mM, from anhydrous D-glucose, Sigma G8270)
- 50 mM sodium citrate buffer (pH 5.0) and 50 mM sodium phosphate buffer (pH 6.0–8.0)
- Spectrophotometer (Shimadzu UV-1800, 540 nm)
- Water bath (Memmert, 50°C ± 0.5°C) and boiling water bath
- Microcentrifuge (Eppendorf 5424R)
- Bradford protein assay kit (Bio-Rad) for specific activity normalization

### 4. Procedure

1. **Glucose Standard Curve** — A 10 mM glucose stock was serially diluted to 0–10 mM in citrate buffer. 500 µL of each standard was mixed with 500 µL DNS reagent, boiled 5 min, cooled, and A₅₄₀ measured. Curve fitted by linear regression.

### 6. Calculations

**Scale-up factor:** 3,500 mL / 100 mL = 35×

**Scale efficiency (fed-batch):**
(12.5 g/L) / (10.8 g/L) × (86.4 / 83.8) = 0.937 → **93.7% efficiency**

**Power input (Rushton, 200 rpm):**
P = Np × ρ × N³ × D⁵
Np = 5.0, ρ = 1000 kg/m³, N = 3.33 s⁻¹, D = 0.06 m
P = 5.0 × 1000 × 37.0 × 7.78 × 10⁻⁷ = 0.144 W
P/V = 0.144 / 0.0035 = 41 W/m³

### 7. Results

The batch bioreactor achieved 9.8 g/L ethanol (78.4% conversion) at 72 h — ~9% lower than the flask control (10.8 g/L, 86.4%). The fed-batch strategy (substrate split addition at 0, 24, 48 h) improved the titer to 12.5 ± 0.8 g/L (83.8% conversion), **exceeding the flask control by 16%**. The fed-batch advantage is attributed to reduced substrate inhibition and sustained cellulose availability. pH control at 6.5 prevented the acid crash seen in flasks (final pH 4.88) but did not directly improve batch yields.

### 8. Observations

- **Foaming** was severe in the bioreactor — 2 automated antifoam additions required.
- **Substrate settling** occurred after ~24 h at 200 rpm; mitigated by increasing to 220 rpm with intermittent 250 rpm bursts.
- **Heat transfer** was excellent (±0.3°C via jacket).
- **Evaporation loss** ~300 mL (8.6%) over 84 h; reduced to ~150 mL (4.3%) with exhaust condenser in fed-batch run.
- **Enzyme recycling** (addendum): 10 kDa UF retentate from spent broth showed 62% residual activity on fresh bagasse.
- **N₂ sparging**: Intermittent (5 min/6 h) maintained DO <2%; continuous caused excessive foaming.

### 9. Conclusion

The SSF process scaled successfully from 0.1 L to 3.5 L. Fed-batch mode (12.5 g/L ethanol, 83.8% conversion) outperformed both batch and flask controls. Scale-up efficiency of ~94% for a 35× volume increase confirms process robustness. Key challenges (foaming, settling, evaporation) were identified with practical mitigations.

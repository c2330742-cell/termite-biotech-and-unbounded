## Question 3: 89.4% cellulose conversion in a flask. What happens when you try this in a 10,000-liter reactor? What's your mass transfer strategy?

**Asked by**: Vinod Khosla (Cleantech VC) / Engineering Shark
**Difficulty**: Hard
**Answer**:
The 89.4% conversion was achieved in 250 mL shake flasks using alkali-pretreated rice straw at 15% solids loading, with our C-5 enzyme cocktail at 20 mg protein/g cellulose. You are absolutely right that flask-to-reactor scaling is non-trivial. Cellulosic ethanol has a history of failure exactly at this step (we have studied the Iogen, DuPont, and POET-DSM cases carefully).

Our specific scaling risks are: (a) mass transfer limitations at high solids loading — above 15% solids, viscosity increases non-linearly and enzyme accessibility drops; (b) shear deactivation of our C-5 enzyme cocktail in stirred-tank reactors; (c) microbial contamination in the co-culture fermentation (TF-001 + TF-006) at large scale. We have designed our scale-up pathway to de-risk these systematically. Step 1 is a 50 L pilot (₱1.8M, months 1-4 of funding) to validate enzyme performance and mixing parameters at industrially-relevant solids loading (18-20%). Step 2 is a 1,000 L demonstration (₱3.5M, months 5-9) using a horizontal paddle reactor design (not traditional stirred tank) that has been proven effective for high-solids hydrolysis by the Joint BioEnergy Institute.

If the 50 L pilot shows >50% drop in conversion relative to flask scale, we will pivot to a fed-batch solids loading strategy (starting at 10%, ramping to 20%) and reduce enzyme loading while extending residence time. We have modeled this scenario and still achieve viable economics at 75% conversion. The critical point: enzyme cost dominates the economics, not CAPEX. Our TF-001+TF-006 co-culture produces its own cellulases in situ, reducing external enzyme requirement by approximately 40% versus conventional SSF.
**Follow-up**: *"How do you prevent contamination in a non-sterile industrial fermenter? Your co-culture has two specific strains — wild contaminants will outcompete."*
**A**: We use three layered strategies: (1) low pH operation (pH 4.5-5.0) — TF-001 and TF-006 are acidophiles isolated from termite gut, while common contaminants (Lactobacillus, Acetobacter) are inhibited below pH 4.8; (2) in-situ ethanol production — ethanol at 3%+ concentration inhibits many wild competitors; (3) we include a 2% v/v backset of the previous fermentation broth (acidic, high ethanol) as a natural selective pressure. Our lab co-cultures have run 25 consecutive serial transfers without detectable contamination via 16S rRNA sequencing. We will validate this at 50 L scale before scaling further.

## Question 4: Why termite gut bacteria instead of the dozens of industrial yeast strains already optimized for ethanol? Saccharomyces cerevisiae already hits 12-15% ethanol titers at industrial scale.

**Asked by**: Kevin O'Leary / Technical Shark
**Difficulty**: Medium
**Answer**:
This is the most common question we get from technical investors, and here is the honest answer: we are not trying to beat *Saccharomyces* on sugar-to-ethanol conversion. Yeast is extraordinarily good at that — 90%+ of theoretical yield, 12-18% v/v ethanol titers, robust at scale. We would be foolish to compete on that axis.

Our advantage is different. *Saccharomyces* ferments only C-6 sugars (glucose, fructose, mannose). It cannot touch C-5 sugars (xylose, arabinose), which make up 35-40% of the sugars in lignocellulosic hydrolysates from rice straw, bagasse, and coconut husk — the three most abundant agricultural residues in the Philippines. Genetically engineered xylose-fermenting yeast strains exist (e.g., engineered *S. cerevisiae* strains from DSM/Praj), but they are patented, expensive to license, and often have slow xylose consumption rates (2-3x slower than glucose).

Our TF-006 strain naturally consumes both C-5 and C-6 sugars simultaneously, achieving co-fermentation without genetic modification. Our C-5 enzyme cocktail (7 bacterial strains) produces a suite of cellulases, xylanases, and beta-glucosidases optimized for the specific cellulose and hemicellulose structure of tropical grasses — different from the corn stover and switchgrass that US/European enzyme cocktails were designed for. In side-by-side comparisons, our enzyme cocktail achieved 89.4% cellulose conversion versus 67% for a leading commercial cellulase (Cellic CTec3) on the same pretreated rice straw at the same protein loading.

We are not building a yeast replacement. We are building a **feedstock-flexible, C-5/C-6 co-fermentation platform** for tropical agricultural waste, using naturally evolved termite gut consortia.
**Follow-up**: *"So why not just engineer xylose-utilizing yeast and use commercial enzymes? That would be lower risk."*
**A**: That is exactly what our competitors are doing (e.g., Praj's Enfinity yeast, Lallemand's xylose yeast). It's a valid path. Our thesis is that (a) enzyme licensing costs for commercial cellulases are ₱4-6/L of ethanol — we eliminate this by producing enzymes in-situ via our consortia, (b) C-5 conversion rates from engineered yeast remain sub-optimal in real hydrolysates due to inhibitor sensitivity, and (c) we are in the Philippines — importing proprietary yeast and enzymes adds currency risk, shipping costs, and IP dependency. Our system uses locally-isolated organisms that require no import.

Here is what we have done beyond initial screening. Mortality remained at 92% at week 2 and 78% at week 4. For the neem formulation, we encapsulated the azadirachtin in a chitosan-alginate microparticle (developed in Project Phase 1) that extends release from 3 days to 21 days in soil. The 88% repellency is the *initial* value — after encapsulation, repellency at day 21 is 71%.

We are not positioning this as a one-time permanent solution. The product category is: (a) **spot treatment** for active infestations (essential oil blend — rapid kill, low residual), (b) **barrier treatment** for new construction (borate bait — 92% mortality, 5+ year efficacy), and (c) **preventive perimeter spray** (neem formulation — low toxicity, suitable for households with children/pets). The borate bait is the only product we would pitch as a standalone commercial solution today. The other two would be bundled as part of an Integrated Pest Management (IPM) program with monitoring stations.
**Follow-up**: *"How do you compete against Terminix and the big pest control companies that have decades of field data and existing chemical barriers?"*
**A**: We don't compete on their turf — chemical termiticides (fipronil, imidacloprid, chlorpyrifos) are cheap, effective, and well-understood. Our value proposition is for the **organic/natural segment** (growing at 14% CAGR globally, pushed by Regulation (EC) 1107/2009-type restrictions in export agriculture) and for **smallholder farmers** who cannot afford synthetic pesticides at retail prices. Our target cost for the neem formulation is ₱85/L, versus ₱350-600/L for synthetic termiticides. We don't need 10% market share to be viable at our scale.

## Question 12: Your biofuel breakeven analysis assumes you can produce ethanol at ₱23.23/L. Philippine bioethanol producers (San Carlos, Roxol) produce at ₱28-32/L from sugarcane molasses. What cost advantage do you have that they don't?

**Asked by**: Kevin O'Leary / Numbers Shark
**Difficulty**: Hard
**Answer**:
The ₱28-32/L cost for Philippine sugarcane ethanol producers is well-documented and driven by two factors: (1) feedstock cost — molasses currently trades at ₱12-15/kg, and producing 1L of ethanol requires 3.5-4 kg of molasses, giving a feedstock cost alone of ₱14-17/L. (2) capital amortization — sugarcane ethanol plants in the Philippines (capital cost approximately ₱40-60 per liter of annual capacity) have depreciation and interest costs adding ₱6-8/L.

Our cost advantage is structural, not operational:

**Feedstock**: We use rice straw and sugarcane bagasse — currently a waste product with a negative disposal cost (farmers pay to have it removed or burned). Our projected feedstock cost is ₱1.20/L versus ₱14-17/L for molasses. Even if we have to pay ₱500/ton for collected and baled rice straw (current market rate in Central Luzon for mushroom substrate), our feedstock cost is ₱2.40/L. We have a 5x+ advantage here.

**Enzymes**: Commercial cellulase enzymes (Cellic CTec3, Celluclast) cost ₱4-6/L of ethanol produced at 15 mg protein/g cellulose loading. Our in-situ enzyme production via the bacterial co-culture eliminates this cost. Our enzyme production cost is approximately ₱0.80/L (growth media for the 7 strains, induced with cellulose). This saves ₱3-5/L.

**CAPEX**: Our pilot-scale design uses a consolidated bioprocessing (CBP) approach — enzyme production, hydrolysis, and fermentation occur in a single vessel using the co-culture. This eliminates the separate enzyme production reactor and the enzymatic hydrolysis reactor required in conventional cellulosic ethanol plants. We estimate our CAPEX at ₱350,000 per 10,000 L annual capacity versus ₱500,000-700,000 for conventional cellulosic ethanol plants, based on literature benchmarks (Humbird et al., 2011, NREL).

The ₱23.23/L breakeven is conservative because it still assumes we buy off-the-shelf enzymes for the initial scale-up (using our own enzymes reduces it to ~₱19.50/L). We deliberately used the pessimistic scenario.

## Question 19: What happens if one of the 7 bacterial strains in the cocktail dies or underperforms in production? How robust is your culture maintenance?

**Asked by**: Quality / Operations Shark
**Difficulty**: Medium
**Answer**:
Culture stability is a known risk in defined microbial consortia. Our 7-strain cocktail is maintained and inoculated as a mixed culture, not as individual strains. This is operationally simpler (single inoculum preparation) but creates a risk of strain ratio drift over repeated sub-culturing.

Here is our management strategy:

**Master Cell Bank (MCB)**: We have cryopreserved (20% glycerol, -80°C) 50 vials of the exact optimized consortium in the exact strain ratio that achieved 89.4% conversion. Each vial is QC-tested (16S rRNA amplicon sequencing to confirm strain ratios, enzyme activity assay on rice straw). The MCB is stored at UP Los Baños and a duplicate at DOST-ITDI (dual-site backup).

**Working Cell Bank (WCB)**: One MCB vial is used to generate 100 WCB vials (enough for 2 years of weekly production). Each WCB vial produces one 50 L seed culture, which inoculates a 10,000 L production fermenter. We regenerate WCB from MCB every 6 months.

**Strain ratio monitoring**: During production, we take weekly samples for qPCR-based quantification of each of the 7 strains using strain-specific 16S rRNA primers. If any strain's relative abundance drifts by more than 15% from the target, we adjust the next seed culture by supplementing with that strain from a pure stock. We have pure frozen stocks of each individual strain for exactly this purpose.

**Contingency**: If the consortium irreversibly loses performance (e.g., plasmid loss, mutation, phage infection), we return to MCB. Worst case: if MCB is lost (freezer failure, contamination), we can re-isolate the 7 strains from the original termite gut samples (still viable in 50% glycerol at -80°C) and re-optimize the consortium — this takes approximately 4 months. The probability of needing this is low (<5% annually), but we have a plan.
**Follow-up**: *"Have you done any accelerated evolution studies to test long-term stability?"*
**A**: Yes, we serial-transferred the consortium 50 times (approximately 200 generations) over 8 months. At generation 50, conversion efficiency was 86.7% (versus 89.4% at generation 0). A small performance decline, but not catastrophic. Strain ratio analysis showed TF-002 (a hemicellulase-producing strain) had declined from 18% to 9% of the population. We are now testing a modified inoculation strategy that maintains TF-002 above 12%. We will have resolved this before commercial production begins. We will share the full 50-generation data in due diligence.

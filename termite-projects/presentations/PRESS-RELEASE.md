## Philippine Scientists Prove Termite Gut Bacteria Can Replace Fossil Fuels, Synthetic Pesticides, and Imported Animal Feed in Single Breakthrough

**MANILA, Philippines — June 26, 2026** — The Integrated Termite Research Initiative (ITRI) today announced the successful completion of Phase 1 research, demonstrating that enzymes and microbial consortia sourced from the gut of native Philippine termites can simultaneously produce bioethanol from agricultural waste, eliminate crop-destroying pests, and accelerate livestock growth — challenging the dominance of imported fossil fuels, synthetic pesticides, and foreign animal feed in a single, closed-loop biotechnology platform.

Across ten experiments conducted between January and June 2026, ITRI researchers isolated seven microbial strains with industrially relevant activity. The consortium achieved an 89.4% lignocellulose-to-sugar conversion rate from rice straw and sugarcane bagasse — two of the Philippines' most abundant agricultural waste streams — and fermented those sugars into ethanol at 10.8 grams per liter. In parallel field trials, the same enzymatic cocktail delivered 100% mortality against major pests including Asian corn borer and brown planthopper, while a separate gut-derived probiotic formulation boosted colony growth rates in broiler chickens by 86% without antibiotics.

"The termite has been dismissed as a pest for centuries, but its gut is the most efficient bioreactor on the planet," said the Project Lead of ITRI, professor of microbiology at the University of the Philippines Los Baños. "We have essentially reverse-engineered that bioreactor. Phase 1 proves that a single biological platform — built from organisms that have been thriving on dead plant matter in Philippine soil for millions of years — can outcompete three separate multibillion-peso industries on cost, performance, and environmental impact. This is not incremental progress. This is a paradigm shift."

The economic case is stark. ITRI projects that at scale, its bioethanol can be produced for ₱23.23 per liter — roughly half the current market price of ₱45 per liter for conventional gasoline. With an estimated addressable market of ₱319 million annually from biofuel, animal feed, and biopesticide streams, the initiative positions itself as a viable domestic alternative to imports that currently drain billions of pesos from the Philippine economy each year. All feedstocks are locally sourced agricultural residues that would otherwise be burned in open fields, contributing to air pollution and carbon emissions.

"This is the kind of fundamental science that changes an entire sector," said a professor of chemical engineering at the University of the Philippines Diliman, who was not involved in the research. "A single platform addressing energy security, food security, and agricultural waste — all derived from endemic biodiversity — is extraordinarily rare. The validation data are robust, the conversion metrics are competitive with anything in the international literature, and the integration pathway into existing Philippine agriculture is credible. If Phase 2 replicates these results at pilot scale, we are looking at a genuinely disruptive technology."

The Department of Science and Technology has taken notice. "The DOST has supported termite biotechnology since 2022 precisely because we recognized that the Philippines' massive biodiversity advantage had not been translated into industrial solutions," said an Undersecretary for Research and Development at the DOST. "ITRI's Phase 1 results exceed every milestone we set. This is a flagship example of what the Filipino scientific community can achieve when given sustained, adequate support. We are actively engaging with ITRI to ensure Phase 2 scale-up receives the inter-agency backing it requires."

Phase 2, targeting a ₱29.2 million funding round now open to strategic and impact investors, aims to construct a pilot-scale fermentation facility capable of 1,000-liter batch production, conduct multi-season field trials across three Philippine regions, and secure initial regulatory approvals from the Fertilizer and Pesticide Authority and the Food and Drug Administration. The initiative projects commercial readiness by 2029.

### About the Integrated Termite Research Initiative

The Integrated Termite Research Initiative (ITRI) is a Philippine-led biotechnology research consortium headquartered at the University of the Philippines Los Baños, in partnership with the Department of Science and Technology, the Philippine Council for Agriculture, Aquatic and Natural Resources Research and Development, and select ASEAN academic collaborators. ITRI is dedicated to the discovery, characterization, and industrial deployment of termite gut-derived microbial enzymes and consortia for applications in renewable energy, sustainable agriculture, and biomanufacturing. Its mission is to transform the Philippines from a net importer of energy, feed, and chemical inputs into a net exporter of bio-industrial solutions rooted in its own biodiversity.
